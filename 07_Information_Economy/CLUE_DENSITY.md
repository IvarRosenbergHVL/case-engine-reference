# Clue Density

Clue Density describes how many investigation-relevant clues appear within a document, page, section, or artifact.

## Purpose

Clue Density helps control how rewarding and demanding a document feels.

A document with too few clues may feel empty. A document with too many clues may feel artificial or overwhelming.

## Definition

A clue is a player-facing information unit that can support observation, interpretation, hypothesis formation, elimination, contradiction, or confirmation.

Clue Density is the number of such clues per unit of material.

## Clue categories

Clues MAY include:

- evidence exposures
- timestamp details
- location details
- object references
- relationship signals
- source reliability signals
- contradictions
- omissions
- context hints
- suspicious absences
- corroborating details

## Density levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| Very low | Mostly framing or atmosphere. |
| Low | One clue or subtle clue cluster. |
| Medium | Several useful clues. |
| High | Many clue-bearing details. |
| Very high | Dense investigative material requiring careful analysis. |

## Clue density vs information density

Information Density includes all meaningful information.

Clue Density focuses only on information with investigative function.

A document can have high Information Density but low Clue Density if it contains realistic procedural detail with few useful clues.

## Normative requirements

Clue Density SHOULD match document role and target difficulty.

Critical clues SHOULD NOT all appear in high-density documents.

A case SHOULD vary Clue Density across artifacts.

A document with very high Clue Density SHOULD be reviewed for single-document solution risk.

## Validation questions

- How many clues does this document contain?
- Are the clues clustered or spread?
- Does the density feel natural for the document type?
- Are critical clues buried among too many minor clues?
- Does this document carry too much of the solution?
