# Visibility

Visibility describes how easy it is for players to notice a clue or information unit.

## Purpose

Evidence strength and evidence visibility are different.

A clue may be very strong if noticed, but difficult to notice. Another clue may be easy to notice but weak in what it proves.

Visibility gives designers a way to control difficulty without changing the underlying truth.

## Definition

Visibility is the likelihood that attentive players will notice a clue during normal archive inspection.

Visibility does not measure how important the clue is or how easy it is to interpret.

## Visibility levels

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hidden in noise | Present but easy to overlook. |
| 2 | Subtle | Mentioned or shown once without emphasis. |
| 3 | Noticeable | Clear to attentive readers. |
| 4 | Prominent | Easy to notice because of placement or formatting. |
| 5 | Obvious | Very difficult to miss. |

## Visibility factors

Visibility MAY be affected by:

- placement
- formatting
- document role
- visual contrast
- repetition
- proximity to related clues
- page density
- table structure
- image quality
- reader expectation

## Visibility vs explicitness

Visibility describes whether players notice the clue.

Explicitness describes how directly the clue states its meaning.

A clue can be visible but indirect, or hidden but explicit once found.

## Normative requirements

Critical clues SHOULD have visibility appropriate to the declared difficulty.

A solution-locking clue SHOULD NOT have visibility level 1 unless it has redundant support elsewhere.

Visibility SHOULD be planned as part of evidence exposure.

Rendering SHOULD NOT accidentally reduce visibility below intended level.

## Validation questions

- Is this clue likely to be noticed?
- Is its visibility appropriate to its criticality?
- Does rendering preserve visibility?
- Is a critical clue hidden only by noise?
- Does the case provide redundant paths if visibility is low?
