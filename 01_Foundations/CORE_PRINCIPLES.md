# Core Principles

The Case Engine Reference is built on a small set of principles. These principles govern all later models, rules, patterns, schemas, tests, and rendering decisions.

## Principle 1 — Information before documents

A case SHALL be modeled as structured information before it is rendered as documents.

Documents are views over information. They are not the primary model.

## Principle 2 — Truth is hidden

The complete objective truth SHALL remain hidden from players.

The truth model exists for generation, validation, repair, and facilitator material.

## Principle 3 — Evidence is distributed

Important information SHOULD be distributed across multiple independent materials.

No single player-facing document SHOULD contain enough information to solve the case alone.

## Principle 4 — Perception differs from truth

Characters do not know the whole truth.

Characters may misremember, lie, misunderstand, conceal, exaggerate, minimize, or interpret events incorrectly.

## Principle 5 — Discovery is designed

A case is not only a set of facts. It is also a sequence of possible player realizations.

The engine SHOULD model how player hypotheses may change over time.

## Principle 6 — Ambiguity must be fair

Ambiguity SHOULD create discussion, not randomness.

A wrong theory may be plausible early, but the archive SHOULD contain enough material to eliminate it later.

## Principle 7 — Validation is part of generation

A generated case is incomplete until it has been validated.

Validation SHALL inspect consistency, solvability, suspect balance, evidence redundancy, document quality, and spoiler safety.

## Principle 8 — Rendering must preserve design intent

Printable documents, images, folders, and facilitator packs are not neutral output. Rendering choices affect player understanding.

A renderer SHOULD preserve document identity, evidence role, and spoiler boundaries.

## Principle 9 — Facilitator truth is separate

Facilitator material SHALL be separated from player-facing material.

The solution pack SHOULD distinguish confirmed facts, likely inferences, and unresolved uncertainties.

## Principle 10 — Variation must be structural

A unique case is not merely a case with different names.

Variation SHOULD affect motive structure, evidence distribution, discovery path, document mix, suspect balance, setting, and hidden relationships.
