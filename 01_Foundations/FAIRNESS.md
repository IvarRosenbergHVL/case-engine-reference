# Mystery Fairness

Fairness is the property that the intended solution can be justified from the player-facing materials.

## Definition

A case is fair when players can reach the intended solution through reasonable interpretation of the provided archive.

Fair does not mean easy.

Fair does not mean obvious.

Fair means that the case does not depend on hidden author knowledge, facilitator explanation, or internet research to solve its core question.

## Requirements

A fair case SHOULD provide:

- enough evidence to support the intended solution
- enough evidence to eliminate major wrong theories
- enough context to interpret specialized facts
- enough redundancy to survive missed clues
- enough ambiguity to support discussion

## Outside knowledge

Outside knowledge MAY enrich a player's experience.

Outside knowledge MUST NOT be required to understand a critical fact.

If a case depends on specialized knowledge, that knowledge SHOULD be embedded naturally in the archive through context documents such as newspapers, safety bulletins, manuals, brochures, or expert notes.

## Fair ambiguity

Fair ambiguity allows multiple interpretations early.

Unfair ambiguity occurs when the correct interpretation depends on information unavailable to the players.

## Fair misdirection

A red herring is fair when it later becomes explainable.

A red herring is unfair when it is random, disconnected, or impossible to evaluate.

## Validation questions

A fairness validator SHOULD ask:

- Can the intended solution be justified using only player-facing material?
- Does every critical fact have redundant support?
- Is required domain knowledge embedded in the archive?
- Are false leads eliminable?
- Are remaining uncertainties clearly distinguished from required conclusions?
